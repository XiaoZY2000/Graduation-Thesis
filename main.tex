\begin{abstract}
	Recommendation systems play an important role in helping users discover relevant
	items in many applications, such as e-commerce platforms, streaming services,
	and social media.
	As online content continues to grow rapidly, building effective recommendation
	systems has become increasingly important.

	However, traditional recommendation systems often suffer from data sparsity and
	cold-start problems, which limit their performance.
	Cross-domain recommendation has therefore been proposed as a way to improve
	recommendation quality by utilizing information from related domains.
	Most existing cross-domain recommendation methods, however, assume that users or
	items overlap across domains during training, an assumption that is often
	unrealistic in practice.
	In addition, user preferences are usually represented as fixed vectors, which
	makes it difficult to capture the uncertainty and diversity of real user
	interests.

	In this thesis, we propose a cross-domain recommendation framework that models
	user preferences as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) under a non-overlapping
	training setting.
	By representing user preferences as probability distributions, the proposed
	framework can better capture diverse and uncertain user interests.
	Based on these distributional representations, optimal transport is applied at
	the distribution level to align user preferences across domains and enable
	knowledge transfer at inference time when cross-domain user correspondence is
	available.
	Importantly, the proposed method does not rely on overlapping users or items
	during training, and can flexibly incorporate overlapping user information at
	inference time when such information is available.

	Extensive experiments on multiple Amazon Review datasets show that the proposed
	approach consistently outperforms strong single-domain baselines and
	representative cross-domain recommendation methods.
	Additional ablation studies further confirm the effectiveness of distribution-
	based user preference modeling and the benefits of leveraging source-domain
	information for improving target-domain recommendation performance.
\end{abstract}
